CHAPTER 1: BASIC GEOMETRY INN \( \mathbf{ \mathbb{N}_0 \times \mathbb{N}_0 } \)

SHAPING THE SUPPORT OF COUNTEREXAMPLES IN \( K[x, y] \)

In this chapter we first give a detailed description of the geometry of the support of polynomials in \( K[x,y] \) and the relation with the plane Jacobian conjecture. Then we introduce our main tools: directions and leading terms \( \ell_{\rho\sigma}(P) \) corresponding to these directions (see Definition 1.4.2), the transformation of the shape of the support by certain automorphisms of \( K[x,y] \), and the very important element \( F \). The main result of this chapter is that if the Jacobian conjecture is false, then \( B \geq 9 \), where \( B := \min\bigl(\gcd(\deg(P),\deg(Q))\bigr) \), and \( (P,Q) \) runs on the counterexamples. This result was already proven by Nagata in [4]. In order to prove \( B \geq 9 \), we consider a counterexample \( (P,Q) \) such that \( B = \gcd(\deg(P),\deg(Q)) \). We transform this counterexample into a standard \( (m,n) \)-pair in \( K[x,y] \) (see Definition 1.7.2). For the transformed pair there exist \( (\rho,\sigma) \)-homogeneous elements (see Definition 1.4.4) \( R \) and \( F \) satisfying very strong conditions (see Theorem 1.8.1). The supports of \( R \) and \( F \) satisfying these conditions, with minimum \( \deg(R) \), are shown in the Figure 1.1.

\(\deg(R) = 9 \), which implies \(B \geq 9 \). The key point of the proof is the transformation of the minimal counterexample into a standard \( (m,n) \)-pair, which needs the most amount of work. This transformation is carried out using automorphisms of the algebra \( K[x,y] \), which alter the support of the polynomials—subsets of \( \mathbb{N}_0 \times \mathbb{N}_0 \)—through basic geometric operations on the plane. The shaping of the support depends on the multiplicity of the linear factors in the polynomials \( \ell_{\rho\sigma}(P) \), which are strongly conditioned by the multiplicity of these linear factors in the \( (\rho,\sigma) \)-homogeneous polynomial \( F \) (see Proposition 1.5.5). This underlines the importance of the element \( F \), which is the crucial new tool of our whole approach.

1.1 Polynomials in two variables and their support

Polynomials in two variables with coefficients in a field \( K \) are expressions of the form \[P = P(x,y) = \sum_{i,j \in \mathbb{N}_0} a_{ij} x^i y^j,\] with each \( a_{ij} \in K \) and only finitely many \( a_{ij} \neq 0 \). For example, \[P = 3x^3 + 2x^2 + 4xy + 7y^2 + 2y - 8\] has \( a_{30} = 3 \), \( a_{20} = 2 \), \( a_{11} = 4 \), \( a_{02} = 7 \), \( a_{01} = 2 \), \( a_{00} = -8 \) and \( a_{ij} = 0 \), for all other indices \( (i,j) \). By definition, the support of a polynomial \( P = \sum_{i,j \in \mathbb{N}_0} a_{ij} x^i y^j \), is the set \[\operatorname{Supp}(P) := \{(i,j) : a_{ij} \neq 0\},\] which is a finite set contained in first quadrant of the plane. If we consider the polynomial \( P \) as a function \( P\colon \mathbb{N}_0 \times \mathbb{N}_0 \to K \), given by \( P(i,j) := a_{ij} \), then our definition of support coincides with the usual definition of the support of a function, which is the subset of the domain consisting of the elements which are not mapped to zero. For example, the support of the polynomial P above is shown in Figure 1.2.

The algebra of polynomials in two variables over the field \( K \) is called \( K[x,y] \). It is a vector space over \( K \) with basis \( \{x^i y^j : (i,j) \in \mathbb{N}_0^2\} \). The multiplication is given by \[x^i y^j \cdot x^k y^l := x^{i+k} y^{j+l}\] on elements of the basis, and then extended by bilinearity to arbitrary polynomials. If one of \( P \) or \( Q \) is a monomial, then the support of the product is the sum of the supports.
